LIR Consolidation test (ASTM D2435)

The test consist of applying a series of incremental loads to a soil specimen in an oedometer and measuring the settlement as a function of time.
Is the ratio between the increase/decrease of axial stress to the current total axial stress.
\( LIR= \Delta \sigma_v / \sigma_v \)
  • Too large \(LIR \implies \) low resolution to determine \(\sigma'_p\)
  • Too small \(LIR \implies\) lengthy test
  • Too long \(LID \implies\) large creep effects
  • To short \(LID \implies\) not reaching EOP
LID and LIR must be carefully selected to prevent the issues mentioned above. The following are suggested in the ASTM D2435:
  • Loading LIR=1
  • Unloading LIR=-2
  • \(LID=\) 24 hr.

  2. After completion Download and backup data.
  3. Umount the sample and measure weight and moisture.

Machine deflections

Machine deflections are the result of applying loads to specimen. The reactions in the frame produce additional deformation that must be corrected is larger than 0.01%.
  1. Machine deflections move reference plane.
  2. Only dependent of frame stiffness.
  3. Modern consolidometers do this automatically.

Defining the loading schedule

To define the loading schedule we need the following information:
  1. Estimate the in-situ effective stress.
  2. Estimate the preconsolidation pressure.
  3. Estimate ultimate effective stress.
According to the ASTM D2435, the maximum vertical stress should be sufficiently large to provide either:
  1. Have at least three points in the virgin curve.
  2. Eight times the preconsolidation pressure.

Data post-processing

Two methods to choose void ratios are available (ASTM D2435):
  1. Method A: The void ratio is measured at the end of load increment (EOI).
  2. Method B: The void ratio is measured at the end of primary consolidation (EOP).

Calc. of the preconsolidation pressure

Casagrande's method

Becker's method

Uses work as a criterion for determining \(\sigma'_p\).
  • It is less subjective than Casagrande's method.
  • Better than Casagrande's for tests with poorly defined preconsolidation pressure.
  • Read paper: "Work as a criterion for determining in-situ and yield stresses in clay"
The original paper proposed the following discretized method to calculate the work done during oedometer tests.
\( ΔW_{oed}=\left[\cfrac{\sigma'_i+σ'_{i+1}}{2}\right](ε_{i+1}-ε_i) \)

\(\Delta W_{oed} \equiv\) Change of work done during the \(i^{th}\) increment of stress.
\(\varepsilon_i= \delta_i / H_o \equiv\) Engineering axial strain.
\(\delta_i \equiv\) axial deformation.

Morin (1988) proposed a cumulative correction based on current height of sample (\(H_i\)).
\( W_{oed}=\sum^k_{i=1} \left[\cfrac{\sigma'_i+σ'_{i+1}}{2}\right]\left(\cfrac{\delta_{i+1}-\delta_i}{H_i}\right) \cfrac{H_i}{H_k} \)

Determining the coefficient of consolidation

  • Need the time-settlement curve
  • Drainage conditions must be known: Single or double.
  • Need times corresponding to known degrees of consolidation (e.g., \(t_{50}\), \(t_{90}\))
Methods to determine \(C_v\):
  • Casagrande (1940) \(\longrightarrow\) \(t_{50}\)
  • Taylor (1948) \(\longrightarrow\) \(t_{90}\)
  • Robinson and Allam (1996) \(\longrightarrow\) \(t_{22}\)
  • Mesri et al. (1999) \(\longrightarrow\) \(t_{70}\)
  • Feng and Lee (2001) \(\longrightarrow\) \(t_{60}\)

Casagrande (1940)

\( C_v= \cfrac{0.197 H_{dr}^2}{t_{50}} \)

Taylor (1948)

\( C_v= \cfrac{0.848 H_{dr}^2}{t_{90}} \)

Which method to use?

  • Taylor's method produces higher \(C_v\) values than Casagrande's method.
  • Taylor's method is applicable to results that do not show the classic "s" shape curve.
  • Field \(C_v\) is usually higher than the one obtained in laboratory. For this reason, Taylor's method usually gives values closer to the field condition.
  • Both methods are normally used. In practice, you will need to calibrate your \(C_v\) during construction.
Note: You will be able to calculate a \(C_v\) value for each load increment. You can construct a curve of \(C_v\) vs. \(\sigma'_v\) or \(e\).
  • Controlled by void ratio and material stiffness.
  • Demarked change in the virgin compression curve.
  • There are no unique patterns.
  • In practice, use \(C_v\) consistent with stress history of soil.
  • You cal also construct \(m_v\) and \(k\) vs \(\sigma'_v\).

Pros and cons of LIR

You will be able to asses this in detail by yourself in the laboratory practice.

Pros

  • Easy to mount.

Cons

  • Is not back-pressured.
