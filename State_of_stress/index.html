State of stress

Stress is a measure of internal forces acting on a continuum body. At any point in the body, a set of stresses is acting. We call this a state of stress.

\( \boldsymbol{\sigma} \): Is a second order tensor that contains information about the state of stress at a point.
\( \begin{align} \boldsymbol{\sigma} = \begin{bmatrix} \sigma_{xx} & \tau_{xy} & \tau_{xz} \\ \tau_{yx} & \sigma_{yy} & \tau_{yz} \\ \tau_{zx} & \tau_{zy} & \sigma_{zz} \end{bmatrix} \end{align} \)

Stress in 2D

In some situations, the stress state can be represented in 2D. In this case, the stress tensor is reduced to a 2x2 matrix. The following are the most common stress states in 2D:

Plane stress

Strain in one direction is zero

\( \begin{align} \boldsymbol{\sigma} = \begin{bmatrix} \sigma_{xx} & \tau_{xy} & 0\\ \tau_{yx} & \sigma_{yy} & 0\\ 0 & 0 & \sigma_{zz}\\ \end{bmatrix} \end{align} \)

Axysimmetric stress

Symmetric around the z-axis

\( \begin{align} \boldsymbol{\sigma} = \begin{bmatrix} \sigma_{r} & 0 & \tau_{rz}\\ 0 & \sigma_{\theta} & 0\\ \tau_{zr} & 0 & \sigma_{zz}\\ \end{bmatrix} \end{align} \)

Stress acting on a plane

In many cases we will need to find stresses acting at a plane of interest.

Sign convention

  1. Compressive stresses are positive.
  2. Shear stresses acting counter-clockwise are positive.
  3. Angles are positive if they open counter-clockwise.
Solving equilibrium equations, we can find the stresses acting on the plane of interest.
\( \sigma_{\alpha}= \cfrac{\sigma_x + \sigma_y}{2} + \cfrac{\sigma_x - \sigma_y}{2} \cos (2 \alpha) + \tau_{yx} \sin(2 \alpha) \)
\( \tau_{\alpha}= \cfrac{\sigma_x - \sigma_y}{2} \sin (2 \alpha) - \tau_{yx} \cos(2 \alpha) \)
Note that these equations represent a circle for \( 0 \leq \alpha \leq \pi \).
We call this circle the Mohr circle.

Mohr circle

Is graphical representation of a state of stress. It is only valid for 2D stresses.
\(\sigma_m\):
\( \sigma_m= 0.5 (\sigma_x + \sigma_y) \)
\(\sigma_d\):
\( \sigma_d= \sqrt{(\sigma_x - \sigma_y)^2 + 4 \tau_{xy}^2} \)
Note that the point with the star can be plotted using the mean stress and the deviator stress as coordinates.
Using this graphical representation we can find the principal stresses and the stress acting at any plane.
: Are the normal stresses acting on the plane where the shear stress is zero.
\( \sigma_{1,3}= \sigma_m \pm 0.5 \sigma_d \)

Thus, the stresses acting at any point can be calculated as:

\( \sigma_{\alpha}= \sigma_m + 0.5 \sigma_d \cos(2 \alpha) \)


\( \tau_{\alpha}= 0.5 \sigma_d \sin(2 \alpha) \)

Cambridge stress invariants

Cambridge stress invariants are the invariants of the full stress tensor. These invariants are very important and are used to define constitutive models for materials.
\(p\):
\( p= \cfrac{\sigma_x+\sigma_y+\sigma_z}{3} \)
\(q= \):
\( q=1/\sqrt{2} [(\sigma_{x} - \sigma_{y})^2 + (\sigma_{x}-\sigma_{z})^2 +(\sigma_{y}- \sigma_{z} )^2 + 6 \tau_{xy}^2 + 6 \tau_{xz}^2 + 6 \tau_{yz}^2 ]^{\frac{1}{2}}\)

For triaxial conditions \(\sigma_d=q\).

Stress paths

Is a collection of stress states plotted in a stress vs stress plot. Typically, they can be MIT stress paths if \(\sigma_m\) and \(\tau_{max}\) are used; or Cambridge stress paths when \(p\) and \(q\) are used.
